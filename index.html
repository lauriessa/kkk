For millions of Filipinos, the rising cost of basic food is more than just a statistic. It is a daily struggle. Despite national progress, over 17 million people still live below the poverty line and face the constant threat of price volatility. This financial pressure is often exacerbated by external costs, such as fuel inflation. When fuel prices rise, commuters are forced to spend their limited funds on travel rather than essential goods, while the high cost of moving products creates delays in cash flow (Lazaro, 2023). These logistics challenges do not hit every area equally. Because transport costs depend on distance and infrastructure, food prices vary significantly across different regions. This means that a family's ability to afford a meal often depends entirely on where they live, leaving those in remote or high-cost areas even more vulnerable to hunger.

Data-Driven Solutions: Our research tracks essential commodities like rice, meat, poultry, fruits, and vegetables to identify market trends.

Here's an overview of our project.

Motivation

Sudden spikes in food prices make essential goods harder for individuals to afford, but these sharp increases do not always happen at the same time across regions. Economically disadvantaged individuals feel the strongest impact, which can deepen existing inequalities.

Our study, Presyo lang Yumayaman, investigates the timing and impact of food price spikes across the Philippines from 2021 to 2025. By understanding how these price shifts move across different regions, we aim to provide the data needed to stabilize markets and protect vulnerable households.

Problem

Frequent food price spikes are a major problem in the Philippines because they happen at different times across regions, making it harder for the poor to recover.

Solution

Our project delivers a deep-dive analysis into how price hikes directly impact poverty across the Philippines. We use the power of data science to understand market trends and identify the best ways to protect the Filipino community from economic shocks.

By comparing these trends with average household incomes, we determine whether Filipino families can truly afford essential food items. This data allows us to identify gaps in the market and develop smarter strategies for a more stable and affordable food system.

The Big Questions: What We Are Solving

To understand how food costs truly impact Filipino families, our research is driven by two critical questions:

1. Does Every Region Feel the Squeeze at the Same Time?

To what extent do the annual price spikes of selected food commodities coincide across regions between 2021 and 2025, and did this synchronization differ between regions with higher and lower 2023 poverty incidence among the population?

2. Is it the Real Poverty Driver?

Is there a significant relationship between regional price volatility from 2021 to 2025 and the region's poverty index among the population?

Our Big Bets: The Hypotheses We Are Testing

Our research is built on two strategic assumptions that we are testing with data from 2021 to 2025:

  • The Poverty Split: The co-movement of annual surges in selected food items differs significantly across regions and varies according to their 2023 poverty incidence classification.
  • The Volatility Connection: There is a statistically significant association between the magnitude of regional market variability from 2021 to 2025 and the level of deprivation experienced by the population within each region.

Our data.

This data set contains the following data:

2023 Regional Poverty Incidence among Population

The 2023 Regional Poverty Incidence among Population data came from the Philippine Statistics Authority, which is the government agency responsible for collecting, compiling, and publishing official statistics in the Philippines.

Monthly Regional Food Price For selected Commodity from January 2021 to December 2025

The data for Monthly Regional Food Price For Selected Commodity from January 2021 to December 2025 is sourced from the Humanitarian Data Exchange (HDX), a platform managed by the United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA). The dataset contains prices of basic commodities from 2000 to 2025, categorized by regions in the Philippines.

The Commodities Rice, Meat, Beef, Cabbage, Eggplants, Eggs, and Tomatoes

Data Preprocessing

We processed over 91,000 data points from the Humanitarian Data Exchange (HDX) to identify key food price trends across the Philippines. Our preprocessing involved the following steps:

  • Strategic Filtering: Limited the dataset to records from 2021–2025 to reflect the current economic climate.
  • Regional Grouping: Organized the data by region and year to track how price changes moved across the country.
  • Data Intersection: Focused only on commodities consistently recorded across all regions to ensure reliable comparisons.
  • Frequency Ranking: Prioritized commodities based on the Food Consumption Survey from the 2021 Expanded National Nutrition Survey conducted by DOST–FNRI.

Exploratory Data Analysis

Dive into our findings for each research question below.

We transformed our dataset into intuitive plots and graphs to illustrate and easily visualize the complex relationships and trends of price per region. To analyze the synchronization of price spikes across regions, the Pearson correlation coefficient was applied to spike ratios, allowing us to quantify how similarly regions experience price surges over time. The price spike ratio was computed by identifying spikes as prices exceeding the mean plus standard deviation for each food and region, counting the number of such spikes per month, and dividing by the total number of food items to obtain the proportion of commodities experiencing abnormal price increases.

Across the five heatmaps (2021–2025), regional price spike synchronization shows a clear but varying pattern over time.

Figure 1. Correlation of Regional Price Spike Ratios (2021)

In 2021, correlations are generally moderate and uneven, with stronger co-movement observed in some clusters while others remain weak.

Figure 2. Correlation of Regional Price Spike Ratios (2022)

In 2022, synchronization becomes very strong across nearly all regions, indicating widespread, simultaneous price spikes likely driven by a national-level shock.

Figure 3. Correlation of Regional Price Spike Ratios (2023)

In 2023, the pattern becomes more fragmented, with mixed and weaker correlations, suggesting increasing regional divergence.

Figure 4. Correlation of Regional Price Spike Ratios (2024)

Synchronization rises again in 2024, with many regions showing moderately high correlations, though not as uniformly strong as in 2022.

Figure 5. Correlation of Regional Price Spike Ratios (2025)

By 2025, the pattern stabilizes into a more balanced state, with mostly moderate correlations and a few strong regional relationships.

Notably, earlier years show tighter alignment among regions in Luzon, while variability in later years reflects differing dynamics across Luzon, Visayas, and Mindanao. Overall, the results indicate that price spike synchronization is not constant, peaking during systemic shocks and weakening when regional factors play a larger role.

Figure 6. Average Price Synchronization (2021-2025)

This bar chart shows the average synchronization score of price spikes by region and poverty level. Low-poverty regions (green), such as NCR, Region I, Region III, and CAR, generally have higher scores (~0.45–0.48), indicating more synchronized price movements and stronger exposure to national trends. In contrast, many high-poverty regions (red), particularly in Visayas and Mindanao, have lower scores (~0.20–0.30), suggesting more irregular and locally driven price behavior.

The Mann–Whitney U test was used to compare synchronization scores between low- and high-poverty regions. This test ranks all observations from lowest to highest and evaluates whether the ranks of one group are systematically higher or lower than those of the other group.

For this test, the hypotheses are defined as follows:

  • Null Hypothesis: The co-movement or synchronization of price spikes does not differ significantly between regions when grouped by poverty incidence.
  • Alternative Hypothesis: The co-movement or synchronization of price spikes differs significantly between regions based on their poverty incidence classification.

A significance level of α = 0.05 was used. When the p-value is less than 0.05, the null hypothesis is rejected, indicating a statistically significant difference between the two poverty groups.

Results:

Mann–Whitney U Test
U statistic = 61
p-value = 0.0152

The results showed a statistically significant difference between the groups. Since the p-value is less than 0.05, the null hypothesis is rejected. This indicates that synchronization levels differ significantly across poverty classifications, with low-poverty regions exhibiting higher synchronization than high-poverty regions.

To help visualize and better understand the relationship between the variables, a scatter plot was used to display the association between poverty incidence and food price volatility across regions. Each point in the graph represents a region, positioned according to its corresponding poverty level (x-axis) and volatility measure (y-axis).

The scatter plot below shows a downward-sloping trendline, indicating a negative relationship between the two variables. The data points follow a relatively consistent downward pattern, implying that the relationship is not random. While some variation exists across regions, the overall distribution supports the presence of an inverse association between poverty and volatility. Furthermore, the pattern appears approximately linear, as the points cluster around the fitted line without significant curvature or directional shifts.

Figure X. Relationship Between Poverty Incidence and Food Price Volatility

As the data consists of cross-sectional regional observations, we analyzed the relationship between food price volatility and poverty incidence using both Pearson and Spearman correlation tests. These tests are appropriate for determining whether a statistically significant association exists between two variables.

The Pearson correlation test measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship, while the Spearman correlation test assesses a monotonic relationship, which does not require linearity and is more robust to non-normal data and outliers. Using both methods ensures that the results are consistent and reliable.

This approach aligns with the objective of the study: to determine whether variations in food price volatility are associated with differences in poverty levels across regions.

For both tests, the hypotheses are defined as follows:

  • Null Hypothesis: There is no relationship between food price volatility and poverty incidence
  • Alternative Hypothesis: There is a relationship between food price volatility and poverty incidence

A significance level of α = 0.05 was used. When the p-value is less than 0.05, the null hypothesis is rejected, indicating a statistically significant relationship.

Results:

Pearson Correlation Test Spearman Correlation Test
P-value = 0.0008 0.0009
r = -0.7320 ρ = -0.7304

Since both p-values are less than the significance level, we reject the null hypothesis in both cases. This indicates, with 95% confidence, that there is a statistically significant relationship between food price volatility and poverty incidence.

Additionally, both correlation coefficients are negative (r = -0.7320; ρ = -0.7304), indicating that the relationship is inverse in direction.
